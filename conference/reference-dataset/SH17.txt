A B S T R A C T
Workplace accidents continue to pose significant human safety risks, particularly in the construction and
manufacturing industries. The necessity for effective Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) compliance has
become increasingly paramount. We focus on developing non-invasive techniques based on the Object
Detection (OD) and Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The aim is to detect and verify the proper use
of various types of PPE such as helmets, safety glasses, masks, and protective clothing. This study proposes
the SH17 Dataset, consisting of 8,099 annotated images containing 75,994 instances of 17 classes collected
from diverse industrial environments, to train and validate the OD models. We have trained state-of-the-art OD
models for benchmarking, and initial results demonstrate promising accuracy levels with You Only Look Once
(YOLO)v9-e model variant exceeding 70.9% in PPE detection. The validation of the model across cross-domain
datasets indicates that integrating these technologies can substantially enhance safety management systems.
This approach offers a scalable and efficient solution for industries seeking to comply with human safety
regulations while safeguarding their workforce. The dataset is available at https://github.com/ahmadmughees/
sh17dataset.
1. Introduction
Occupational Safety and Health (OSH) is a vital multidisciplinary
domain focused on ensuring safe working environments and mitigating
risks to worker health. It encompasses the prevention of work-related
injuries, accidents, and illnesses by systematically managing work-
place hazards and promoting health-conscious practices. In industrial
manufacturing, PPE plays a pivotal role in protecting workers from
various occupational hazards. The importance of PPE lies in its ability
to significantly reduce the risks of injuries and illnesses resulting from
exposure to hazardous conditions such as toxic chemicals, extreme
temperatures, and mechanical dangers.
Extensive research has demonstrated that PPE serves as an essential
last line of defense in safeguarding workers when other safety measures
are inadequate [1,2]. Manufacturing industries have made considerable
efforts to ensure workforce safety by mandating the use of PPE in
their facilities, aiming to minimize injury-related incidents that can lead
to substantial financial losses. Initially, human inspectors were tasked
with monitoring PPE compliance at key points throughout the facility.
However, this responsibility has increasingly been augmented by Inter-
net of Thing (IoT) systems, which employ sensor-based methodologies
to automate the verification of PPE compliance [3,4].

The advent of Machine Learning (ML) and Computer Vision (CV)
systems has prompted a shift towards the development of fully non-
invasive solutions for PPE compliance monitoring in manufacturing
environments [5,6]. These advancements underscore the potential for
more efficient and precise safety monitoring systems. In accordance
with the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) guide-
lines [7], Isalovic et al. [8] classified PPE into five categories based
on the body regions they are designed to protect: (1) Head, (2) Upper
Body, (3) Hands, (4) Feet, and (5) Whole Body. Typical examples of
PPE include helmets, safety glasses, face shields, earmuffs, safety vests,
gloves, and safety shoes, each tailored to mitigate specific hazards.
While proper selection, usage, and maintenance of PPE are crit-
ical for ensuring worker safety, recent studies [9–12] have increas-
ingly utilized ML-based Object Detection (OD) models to detect PPE
items across various environments, from construction sites to chemical
plants. However, these studies focus on detecting specific PPE items,
such as helmets or vests, within controlled environments [5]. More-
over, many of these approaches depend on proprietary or small-scale
datasets, which restricts their generalizability and limits their broader
applicability.
To overcome these limitations, this study expands the application
of OD-based methods for PPE compliance by introducing a large-
scale, high-quality, open-source dataset. Our dataset consists of 8099

3.5. Evaluation metrics
Object detection models are evaluated using several metrics to
assess their performance. Common metrics used by Microsoft Com-
mon Objects in Context (MS-COCO) dataset [56] and Pascal VOC
dataset [55] include Precision (P), Recall (R), and Mean Average Pre-
cision (mAP).
Precision (P) measures the proportion of correctly identified objects
among all objects detected by the model and is calculated as
Precision =
True Positive
True Positive + False Positive
Recall (R), on the other hand, quantifies the ability of the model to
detect all relevant objects in the dataset and is computed as
Recall =
True Positive
True Positive + False Positive
Additionally, Mean Average Precision (mAP) is utilized to evaluate
the detection accuracy across all classes. This metric is computed based
on P and R for each class and subsequently averaged to produce an
overall score. The Intersection over Union (IoU) metric is employed to
assess the accuracy of object localization, calculating the overlap be-
tween the ground truth bounding boxes (𝑎 𝑎 ) and the model’s predicted
bounding boxes (𝑎 𝑖 𝑖 𝑎 𝑎 ) as follows:

5. Conclusion
Ensuring human safety in manufacturing environments is a
paramount concern, particularly in maintaining PPE compliance. In this
study, the SH17 dataset was introduced and open-sourced, comprising
8099 annotated images across 17 distinct classes that represent various
PPE items and body parts. This dataset is freely available for research
purposes and aims to advance the field of PPE detection in industrial
settings.
To validate the utility of the SH17 dataset, several models were
benchmarked, with YOLOv9-e achieving the highest performance. Ad-
ditionally, the generalization capability of the trained model was tested
using the Pictor-PPE dataset, with results indicating that the model is

A.2. Person demographics
In addition to the bounding boxes representing the Persons class,
additional tags based on visible features within the images have been
included. While these tags are primarily derived from observable char-
acteristics, some inaccuracies may be present, as visual features are
not consistently reliable indicators. Nonetheless, these tags have been
incorporated to identify and mitigate potential biases related to gender
or ethnic representation. The tags encompass categories such as male,
female, children, and various ethnic backgrounds, including Black,
Brown, White, and Asian. A quantitative analysis of these tags is
provided in Table A.2.

in Table A.3.
